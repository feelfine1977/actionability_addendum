**Title:** A framework to rank genomic alterations as targets for cancer precision medicine: the ESMO Scale for Clinical Actionability of molecular Targets (ESCAT)  

**Authors:** J. Mateo, D. Chakravarty, R. Dienstmann, S. Jezdic, A. Gonzalez-Perez, N. Lopez-Bigas, C.K.Y. Ng, P.L. Bedard, G. Tortora, J.-Y. Douillard, E.M. Van Allen, N. Schultz, C. Swanton, F. André, L. Pusztai

**Year:** 2018

Eligible for inclusion: **Yes**

## How Actionability is Understood

The paper defines *clinical actionability* in the context of genomic alterations for cancer precision medicine as the degree to which a molecular target can guide patient management decisions based on evidence linking the alteration to clinical benefit from a matched therapy. Actionability is tied directly to the strength, type, and context of clinical and preclinical evidence supporting a target–drug match.

## What Makes Something Actionable

A genomic alteration is considered actionable if there is robust evidence that targeting it with a specific therapy leads to improved patient outcomes, such as survival or clinically meaningful benefit. The ESCAT framework grades actionability by:

- The type of study (randomised clinical trial, prospective non-randomised, retrospective, preclinical).

- The observed magnitude of benefit (survival improvement, response rates).

- The applicability across cancer types or specific tumour contexts.

- The maturity and reproducibility of evidence.

## Systematic Features or Dimensions

- **CL (Clarity):** Explicit tier definitions with clear clinical criteria.  

- **CR (Contextual Relevance):** Targets evaluated within specific tumour contexts; acknowledges tumour-type variability.  

- **FE (Feasibility):** Incorporates rarity of alterations and feasibility of trial designs (e.g., basket trials).  

- **TI (Timeliness):** Allows action based on strong non-randomised data for rare alterations without delaying for large RCTs.  

- **EX (Explainability):** Framework rationalises why a target is assigned to a tier.  

- **GA (Goal Alignment):** Prioritises targets to improve patient outcomes and avoid misclassification.

### ESCAT Tiers:

1. **Tier I (Routine use)** – Strong clinical evidence (I-A RCT survival benefit, I-B non-randomised clinical benefit, I-C basket/multiple tumour benefit).  

2. **Tier II (Investigational)** – Likely benefit but more data needed (II-A retrospective, II-B prospective with no survival data).  

3. **Tier III (Hypothetical target)** – Evidence from other tumour types or similar alterations.  

4. **Tier IV (Preclinical evidence)** – Actionability predicted in vitro/in vivo or in silico.  

5. **Tier V (Combination development)** – Response observed but no meaningful survival benefit.  

6. **Tier X (No evidence)** – No clinical or preclinical support for actionability.

## Summary

This paper introduces the ESMO Scale for Clinical Actionability of molecular Targets (ESCAT), a structured, evidence-based framework to prioritise genomic alterations for targeted cancer therapies. It defines actionability as the link between a genomic alteration and proven clinical benefit from a matched therapy, graded into six tiers according to the nature and strength of the supporting evidence. The framework addresses key issues in clarity, contextual relevance, feasibility, timeliness, explainability, and goal alignment, aiming to reduce variability and misinterpretation in clinical sequencing reports. ESCAT serves as a common language for oncologists, researchers, and regulators to guide treatment decisions, promote harmonisation, and facilitate evidence-based adoption of precision medicine.

**Title:** A framework for genomic biomarker actionability and its use in clinical decision making  

**Authors:** Smruti J. Vidwans, Michelle L. Turski, Filip Janku, Ignacio Garrido-Laguna, Javier Munoz, Richard Schwab, Vivek Subbiah, Jordi Rodon, Razelle Kurzrock  

**DOI:** n/a  

**Year:** 2014  

## Eligibility

Eligible for inclusion: **Yes**

## How Actionability is Understood

The paper defines a genomic biomarker as actionable if it is oncogenic and/or differentially expressed on tumor cells, and if a treatment approach can be developed to mitigate its oncogenic potential or exploit it for tumor cell targeting. Actionability is not a binary state but depends on the biomarker’s biological role, therapeutic targetability, and the quality of supporting evidence. The authors emphasize linking biomarker characteristics to clinically meaningful interventions through a structured evaluation process.

## What Makes Something Actionable

A biomarker becomes actionable when:

- It functionally drives malignancy and can be directly targeted by approved or investigational drugs.

- It is part of a pathway that can be targeted (directly or indirectly).

- It is homologous to known actionable biomarkers.

- It is differentially expressed and exploitable for targeted therapy delivery.

- There is robust supporting evidence from guidelines, companion diagnostics, clinical trials, pre-clinical studies, or analogous evidence from genetic diseases.

## Systematic Features or Dimensions

**Mapped to CL, CR, FE, TI, EX, GA:**

- **CL (Clarity):** Explicit categorization of biomarkers by functional role and targetability.

- **CR (Contextual Relevance):** Framework applies evidence to specific cancers and extrapolates to others.

- **FE (Feasibility):** Practical use of approved/investigational drugs and established pathways.

- **TI (Timeliness):** Integration of current FDA/NCCN guidelines and clinical trial availability.

- **EX (Explainability):** Detailed rationale for each category of actionability and evidence mapping.

- **GA (Goal Alignment):** Links biomarker features to therapeutic strategies for patient benefit.

## Summary

This paper presents a structured framework for assessing the clinical actionability of genomic biomarkers in oncology. It introduces two core constructs: “Basis of Actionability” (functional relevance, drug targetability, pathway involvement, biomarker homology, exploitable expression) and “Rationale for Actionability” (evidence tiers from guidelines to pre-clinical and genetic disease data). These constructs help classify biomarkers into categories ranging from validated therapeutic targets to non-actionable findings, guiding both clinical decision-making and research prioritization. The approach balances biological plausibility with evidentiary strength, offering a scalable method for integrating rapidly expanding molecular diagnostic data into cancer care.
